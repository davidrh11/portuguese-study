Palavra a Memorizar — Use Sempre
Palavra essencial · use todos os dias
um bocadinho
"a little bit" / "a short while" / "just a moment"
Diminutive of bocado (a bit/a while). The -inho ending makes it smaller, softer, warmer — and Portuguese use it constantly. It's one of the most natural words you can drop into daily conversation.

Espera um bocadinho. — Wait a moment. (softer than "espera")
Fala um bocadinho mais devagar. — Speak a little more slowly.
Só um bocadinho de vinho, obrigado. — Just a little wine, thank you.
Estou um bocadinho cansado. — I'm a little tired.
Mais um bocadinho? — A little more? (offering food/drink)

Why it matters: saying bocadinho instead of pouco instantly sounds more native. It softens requests, avoids bluntness, and shows you've absorbed how Portuguese actually speaks — not just textbook Portuguese. Your instructor will notice.
Palavra do Dia
Expressão do estado
Estou de rastos!
"I'm exhausted / I'm dragging myself along."
Used when you're completely drained — physically or mentally. More vivid than estou cansado. Literally "I'm crawling along the ground." You'll hear this often in Lisbon.

Context: after a long day, a bad night's sleep, a tough week. Perfectly natural in informal conversation.
Diz-se Assim
Resposta ao como está
Vai-se andando.
"So-so." / "Getting by." / "One keeps going."
The Portuguese equivalent of "can't complain" or "so-so" — used when you're not great but not bad. When someone asks Como está? or Tudo bem? and you want to be honest without being dramatic, this is the answer.

Literally: "one keeps walking." The reflexive vai-se makes it impersonal — it's not just you, it's life in general moving forward. Very Portuguese in its understated resignation.
